## Decision 1: Founding Saddleback Through Market Research (1979–1980)

### Context
In 1979, 25-year-old Rick Warren graduated from Southwestern Baptist Theological Seminary and moved with his wife Kay to the Saddleback Valley of Orange County, California — identified through demographic analysis as one of the fastest-growing communities in the US. Rather than founding a traditional church, he conducted a 12-week door-to-door survey of approximately 500 households asking why people did not attend church and what they would want from one.

### Stated Reasoning
Warren believed the church should remove all barriers preventing unchurched people from engaging. The survey produced a composite profile he called "Saddleback Sam" — an affluent, college-educated professional aged 28–40 with young children, skeptical of organized religion but open to practical guidance. He then designed the entire church's style, programming, and aesthetics to match this profile: casual dress, contemporary music, practical sermon topics, no insider jargon.

> "I'm going to give my life to this church." (Warren's stated vow to God at Saddleback's founding)

The first public service was Easter Sunday, April 6, 1980, at Laguna Hills High School, drawing 205 attendees — the majority of whom reported limited prior involvement in organized religion.

### Observed Outcome
Saddleback grew to average nearly 20,000 weekly attendees, becoming one of the largest churches in America. The seeker-sensitive model became enormously influential: Warren's 1995 book *The Purpose Driven Church* spread the methodology globally and reportedly influenced tens of thousands of churches.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Consistent:** Warren consistently stated his core mission was reaching the unchurched, and the founding methodology directly served that goal.

**Tension worth noting:** The survey-driven approach was criticized by traditional evangelicals for subordinating theological content to consumer preference — effectively letting the "customer" define the product. This became a recurring tension: Warren designed the church around "Saddleback Sam," an affluent suburban profile, which shaped the congregation's demographics and arguably its politics for decades.

## Decision 2: The Reverse Tithe — Giving Away Royalties (2003–2006)

### Context
Warren published *The Purpose Driven Life* in 2002. By 2006, it had sold over 30 million copies and become one of the best-selling nonfiction books in history. The book's commercial success generated what industry insiders estimated to be royalties approaching $100 million over time, with at least $20 percent of the wholesale price going to Warren per unit.

### Stated Reasoning
Warren announced on Larry King Live that he and Kay had always tithed — starting at 10 percent of income on their wedding day, then increasing by 1 percent each year to stretch their faith. After the book's success, he "reversed the tithe": keeping 10 percent and donating 90 percent. He also said he repaid Saddleback Church every dollar of salary he had ever received and stopped taking any salary from the church. He created three foundations to channel the donations:

- **Acts of Mercy** — serving those infected and affected by AIDS
- **Equipping the Church** — training church leaders in developing countries
- **The Global PEACE Fund** — addressing poverty, disease, and illiteracy

### Observed Outcome
Warren became a recognized philanthropist. In 2005, *Time* named him one of "The 100 Most Influential People in the World." The foundations funded significant work in Africa, particularly Rwanda. Warren and Kay became prominent public voices on HIV/AIDS in faith communities.

However, a Tax Policy Center analysis noted: even accounting for the reverse tithe and various donations, Warren "likely pockets well in excess of $1 million a year" from the book's ongoing royalties and speaking fees — a not-insignificant personal income.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Consistent:** Warren has stated repeatedly that money and success are stewardships, not personal rewards. The reverse tithe was a public, verifiable action that aligned with this teaching.

**Tension worth noting:** Critics noted the foundations he created (particularly the PEACE Plan) were closely controlled by Warren and Saddleback, raising questions about whether "giving away" wealth to one's own organizations constitutes the same sacrifice it would be to give to unaffiliated charities. The exact governance of these foundations was not publicly transparent.

## Decision 3: The PEACE Plan — Mobilizing the Global Church Against Poverty (2003–2010s)

### Context
In 2003, Kay Warren read an article on AIDS orphans in Africa and the couple subsequently met with a pastor of a township near Johannesburg, South Africa. This catalyzed the PEACE Plan, an ambitious program to use Saddleback's network to address what Warren called "five global giants": spiritual emptiness, self-serving leadership, poverty, disease, and illiteracy. The acronym stood for: **P**lanting churches, **E**quipping leaders, **A**ssisting the poor, **C**aring for the sick, **E**ducating the next generation.

Warren announced a "second Reformation" vision in 2005:

> "The first reformation of the church 500 years ago was about beliefs. This one is going to be about behavior. The first one was about creeds. This one is going to be about deeds."

In 2005, Rwanda became the first permanent PEACE Plan partner country, with Saddleback sending over 2,000 church members and initiating a national strategy with 600 Rwandan churches.

### Stated Reasoning
Warren argued that the global church — with 2.3 billion members, more than 5 million congregations, and assets in every community on earth — was the most powerful network in the world for addressing poverty and disease. Governments and NGOs could not match the reach or relational trust of local congregations.

He spoke at the United Nations, World Economic Forum in Davos, the African Union, and the Council on Foreign Relations to advocate this approach.

### Observed Outcome
**Achievements:** Saddleback held an annual Global Summit on AIDS and the Church attracting thousands of participants. The PEACE Plan mobilized volunteers and raised awareness of global health crises among evangelical audiences previously focused mainly on domestic issues.

**Failures:** A 2009 study by the National University of Rwanda noted significant weaknesses: lack of cooperation between denominational churches, absence of staff to manage outcome evaluation data, and reluctance to collaborate with secular NGOs and governments. Critics called it "a well-meaning, ambitious, and ultimately doomed attempt to impose from the outside a framework for solving chronic problems."

**Course correction:** Warren spent years on overseas "listening sessions." In 2008, he relaunched as PEACE 2.0, changing the first pillar from "Plant churches" to "Promote reconciliation," emphasizing local church autonomy over external programming, and prioritizing "trade over aid."

**Rwanda partnership controversy (late 2010s):** Warren came under criticism for hosting Rwandan President Paul Kagame at Saddleback. Critics, including former Kagame economic adviser David Himbara, noted Kagame had closed 8,000 churches, imprisoned church leaders, silenced political opposition, and was accused of orchestrating the killing of opponents. Saddleback's response was to issue a press release about "standing in solidarity with Rwanda so that the genocide will never be forgotten" — not addressing the human rights criticisms.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Consistent:** The PEACE Plan represented genuine institutional risk and resource commitment to global poverty, consistent with Warren's stated belief that purpose-driven Christianity must address physical suffering, not just spiritual salvation.

**Inconsistent:** The uncritical partnership with Kagame — whose government suppressed the same church networks Warren claimed to champion — revealed that institutional access and political partnerships sometimes took priority over the stated values of protecting vulnerable communities. Saddleback's non-response to the human rights critique was evasive.

## Decision 4: Proposition 8 Support — and Subsequent Denial (2008–2009)

### Context
California's Proposition 8 (November 2008) asked voters to amend the state constitution to define marriage as "only between a man and a woman," reversing a California Supreme Court ruling allowing same-sex marriage. Two weeks before the election — on October 24, 2008 — Warren issued a video message to his 22,000-member congregation.

### Stated Reasoning (at the time)
In the video, Warren said:

> "Now let me say this really clearly: We support Proposition 8."
> "If you believe what the Bible says about marriage, you need to support Proposition 8."

He framed this as defending "a 5,000-year definition of marriage," not as opposition to gay people personally.

In a December 2008 Beliefnet interview, he elaborated — then made controversial comparisons:

> "I'm opposed to the redefinition of a 5,000-year definition of marriage. I'm opposed to having a brother and sister be together and call that marriage. I'm opposed to an older guy marrying a child and calling that a marriage."

He later apologized for these comparisons, saying he had "unintentionally" given the impression he equated consensual adult same-sex relationships with incest and pedophilia.

### The Denial
In an April 2009 CNN interview with Larry King, Warren made a statement that directly contradicted his documented record:

> "During the whole Proposition 8 thing, I never once went to a meeting, never once issued a statement, never — never once even gave an endorsement in the two years Prop 8 was going."

When Christianity Today confronted Warren with the contradiction, he replied:

> "It was a pastor talking to his own people. I've never said anything about it since. I don't know how you can take one video newsletter to your own church and turn that into — all of a sudden I'm the poster boy for anti-gay marriage."

The video had been published on his public "News & Views" website and picked up by American Family Association's OneNewsNow, making it a public — not private — statement.

### Observed Outcome
The controversy had two peak moments:
1. Immediately after Prop 8 passed, same-sex marriage supporters demonstrated at Saddleback Church.
2. When President-elect Obama named Warren to deliver the inaugural invocation (December 2008), it reignited the controversy. Human Rights Campaign president Joe Solmonese stated Warren's selection "tarnished the view that gay, lesbian, bisexual and transgender Americans have a place at your table."

Warren delivered the inauguration prayer on January 20, 2009, despite protests.

He later expressed regret for making the Prop 8 statement at all — not because of a changed theological position, but because of the political cost: "What I learned from that is that anything I say privately is now public."

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Highly inconsistent:** Warren positioned himself throughout his career as a bridge-builder and civil leader who could maintain relationships across political divides. His false denial to Larry King — made on national television while the video evidence remained publicly accessible — represented a straightforward factual misrepresentation, and was widely noted as such in media coverage. His explanation (that a video posted on his public website was a "private" pastoral communication) was not credible.

This incident reveals a pattern: Warren made politically costly public statements, then retrospectively minimized or denied them to preserve his access to multiple audiences simultaneously.

## Decision 5: Hosting the 2008 Saddleback Civil Forum on the Presidency

### Context
On August 16, 2008, Rick Warren hosted back-to-back interviews of presidential candidates Barack Obama and John McCain at Saddleback Church — their first joint appearance as presumptive nominees. The event was broadcast nationally. Warren framed it as a "civil forum" focused on faith, poverty, HIV/AIDS, climate, and human rights.

### Stated Reasoning
Warren saw the forum as an expression of his "third way" politics — demonstrating that evangelicals cared about more than abortion and gay marriage, and that civil dialogue across political differences was possible. He framed himself as a faith leader with access to both parties who could model constructive engagement.

### The "Cone of Silence" Controversy
At the forum's start, Warren told the audience: "We have safely placed Senator McCain in a cone of silence" — implying McCain was sequestered and could not hear questions posed to Obama. In fact, McCain had not yet arrived at the church; he was in his motorcade for the first 30 minutes of the proceedings.

Warren later admitted to CNN's Rick Sanchez that he had believed McCain was in the cone of silence but subsequently learned otherwise. This meant McCain was operating on the honor system — with no physical controls preventing him from hearing Obama's interview via television or radio during his motorcade.

NBC's Andrea Mitchell raised concerns on *Meet the Press* that McCain's notably immediate, crisp answers on questions identical to those Obama had just answered raised the possibility he had advance knowledge. The McCain campaign refused to confirm that no staff members had briefed him on the questions, saying only that McCain personally had not heard or seen the broadcast.

### Observed Outcome
McCain's performance at the forum was widely praised by conservative media and generally considered superior to Obama's. Warren did not publicly address the integrity implications. The controversy largely faded when Prop 8 and the inauguration took over the news cycle.

The forum is generally credited with elevating Warren's national profile as a political broker — arguably the high-water mark of his public influence.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Partially inconsistent:** Warren staked his forum's credibility on procedural fairness (the "cone of silence"). When he learned the control had not been in place, he did not publicly acknowledge or investigate the breach of fairness he had promised the national audience. His subsequent silence on the matter stood in contrast to his stated commitment to civil, trustworthy public discourse.

## Decision 6: Delivering Obama's Inaugural Invocation (January 2009)

### Context
In December 2008, President-elect Barack Obama selected Warren to deliver the invocation at his January 20, 2009, inauguration — a choice that surprised both camps. Warren had publicly supported Proposition 8 the previous month. LGBT organizations immediately protested.

### Stated Reasoning
Obama defended his choice by noting he had previously been invited to speak at Saddleback despite disagreeing with Warren on gay rights and abortion, arguing: "A sense of purpose larger than ourselves" required the ability to disagree without being disagreeable.

Warren accepted the invitation. He later described it as an opportunity to model that faith leaders could participate in democracy and bridge divides.

### Observed Outcome
Warren delivered an explicitly Christian, inclusive-in-tone invocation that celebrated the first African American president. Critics on the right felt he was conferring legitimacy on a pro-abortion president; critics on the left felt he was conferring legitimacy on someone who had just fought to remove marriage rights from same-sex couples.

The appearance had a dual effect on Warren's reputation:
- It expanded his secular media profile and confirmed him as a national figure.
- It angered portions of his conservative evangelical base who saw it as alignment with an opponent of their values.

When Obama and Romney ran in 2012, Warren cancelled a planned repeat Civil Forum, citing "the lack of civility" in political discourse.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Complex:** Warren genuinely and consistently stated he believed in dialogue across differences. Accepting the inauguration invitation was consistent with that stated value. However, the controversy exposed an inherent tension in his bridge-building strategy: "being at the table" with everyone simultaneously requires that none of your substantive positions be treated as disqualifying — which means each constituency perceives you as minimizing the harm your positions cause them.

## Decision 7: Public Response to Son Matthew's Suicide and Mental Health Advocacy (2013–onward)

### Context
On April 5, 2013, Warren's 27-year-old son Matthew died by suicide after a lifelong struggle with mental illness, including borderline personality disorder, depression, and chronic pain. He had used an illegally purchased firearm. Warren broke the news to Saddleback staff via email.

### Stated Reasoning and Response
Warren's initial email stated: "No words can express the anguished grief we feel right now." He described Matthew as having "a brilliant intellect and a gift for sensing who was most in pain or most uncomfortable in a room," but one who had struggled "from birth with mental illness, dark holes of depression, and even suicidal thoughts."

Rather than retreat into private grief, Warren returned to his pulpit after several months and made mental illness advocacy a central public mission. His stated reasoning: he had received more than 10,000 letters from people with similar experiences, and he believed the church was perpetuating harmful stigma.

His core public argument:

> "There's no shame when any other organ in your body fails, so why do we feel shame if our brain is broken?"
> "Your chemistry is not your character. Your illness is not your identity."
> "If my brain doesn't work and I take a pill, I'm supposed to be ashamed?"

He challenged the 48 percent of evangelicals (per LifeWay Research) who believed prayer and Bible study alone could overcome serious mental illness.

### Actions Taken
- Launched a sermon series on grief at Saddleback.
- Co-hosted *The Gathering on Mental Health and the Church* with the Roman Catholic Diocese of Orange and the National Alliance on Mental Illness.
- Planned to fund a mental health ministry with proceeds from the sale of Matthew's house.
- Became a regular public speaker on mental health and faith.

On faith in the face of tragedy:

> "I never questioned my faith in God. I questioned God's plan. There's a big difference."

### Observed Outcome
The Warrens' advocacy shifted the conversation about mental illness in evangelical communities — a population that had historically treated psychological suffering as primarily a spiritual failure. The work produced measurable institutional change: collaboration with secular mental health organizations, church training programs, and national attention.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Highly consistent:** Warren had always preached that the church should engage human suffering in its totality — physical, emotional, and spiritual. His willingness to use the most intimate imaginable personal loss as a lever for institutional change was consistent with his stated conviction that pastors must model the same vulnerability they ask of their congregations.

This was perhaps the most unambiguously admirable of the decisions documented here: the cause was real, the action was costly, and the stated reasoning matched the observable behavior.

## Decision 8: Ordaining Women as Pastors — and the SBC Expulsion (2021–2023)

### Context
For decades, Saddleback Church was a member of the Southern Baptist Convention, despite notable tensions around its seeker-sensitive approach. The SBC's statement of faith, adopted in 2000, restricted the pastoral role to men. On May 6, 2021, Warren and Saddleback ordained three women who had each served in ministry at the church for over 20 years:

- **Cynthia Petty** — children's ministry since 1998
- **Liz Puffer** — pastoral care since 1994
- **Katie Edwards** — youth ministry since the late 1990s

Warren had reportedly been discussing the decision with church elders for months before informing Petty of her pending ordination in November 2020.

### Stated Reasoning
Warren stated he wanted to "speak up for millions of Southern Baptist women" whose "spiritual gifts, their leadership gifts and talents, are being wasted." He framed the issue as one of church autonomy (consistent with the SBC's historically strong local-church tradition) rather than as a theological departure from conservative Christianity.

He launched a public campaign before the June 2023 SBC annual meeting: dozens of tweets, a website, three videos, an open letter, and a four-page messenger's guide arguing that removing Saddleback violated the fellowship's core principles. He explicitly acknowledged the appeal would likely fail:

> "I wanted to push the conversation that's been stagnant for years."

### Observed Outcome
**February 2023:** The SBC formally ousted Saddleback Church — the denomination's second-largest congregation — over the women pastor ordinations. Saddleback appealed.

**June 2023:** SBC messengers voted 88 percent to uphold the disfellowship, and amended the denomination's constitution to explicitly require "only men" for "any kind of pastor or elder." Five congregations were expelled in total in 2023.

Warren's successor Andy Wood remained at Saddleback. Wood's wife, Stacie Wood, was made a "teaching pastor" — the direct structural cause of the expulsion — and retained that role after the SBC vote.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Consistent:** Warren consistently stated that his ministry was about reaching people and unleashing human potential in service to God. Ordaining women who had served faithfully for over 20 years was consistent with this framing.

**Tension worth noting:** The timing raises interpretive questions. Warren had led Saddleback within the SBC for over 40 years without ordaining women, only making this move as he was in the process of retiring and handing off leadership. It is impossible to know whether the timing reflected genuine theological evolution, a deliberate decision to use his retirement as political cover for change he had long wanted, or something else.

The SBC expulsion did not materially harm Saddleback — the church remained large and financially stable. The cost was reputational (within Southern Baptist circles) rather than existential.

## Decision 9: Stepping Down After 42 Years (2021–2022)

### Context
On June 6, 2021, Warren announced he was stepping down as Saddleback's lead pastor — the same period in which he ordained the first women pastors. He cited a neurological condition, spinal myoclonus, causing tremors and blurred vision that had worsened considerably.

### Stated Reasoning
Warren framed his retirement as fulfillment of a promise: "It was my promise to God and to you, God's people...I'm going to give my life to this church." He said the church had a future that required new leadership.

After evaluating approximately 100 candidates, Warren selected Andy Wood, 40, the lead pastor of Echo Church in San Jose, along with Wood's wife Stacie as teaching pastor. Warren stated: "Kay and I believe so much in this couple...God has prepared and chosen them to take up the baton."

His formal last day was September 12, 2022. He retained a "founding pastor" role.

### Observed Outcome
The transition coincided with (and may have been structurally connected to) the women-pastor ordinations. Wood's wife Stacie becoming teaching pastor was the proximate cause of the SBC expulsion. Whether Warren anticipated or intended this outcome remains unclear.

The transition was structured as gradual, with family meetings and community celebration events — consistent with Warren's stated commitment to institutional continuity.

### Consistency with Stated Values
**Consistent:** Warren consistently taught that leaders should develop their successors and eventually step aside. His multi-year succession planning (announcing in 2021, final transition in 2022) was consistent with those teachings.

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

### Pattern 1: Bridge-Builder Identity vs. Substantive Commitments
Warren consistently sought relationships with both political parties, both theological progressives and conservatives. This produced genuine access and influence (the Civil Forum, the inauguration). It also produced specific inconsistencies: his Prop 8 support alienated the very coalition he claimed to bridge, and his subsequent denial was documented to be false.

### Pattern 2: Large Ambitions, Mixed Follow-Through
The PEACE Plan announced "the most significant thing the church has done in 2,000 years." The Rwanda implementation produced a university-documented failure requiring complete restructuring. Warren's response — acknowledging failure, revising, continuing — shows genuine resilience, but the gap between announcement and execution was significant and consistent across projects.

### Pattern 3: Personal Suffering as Institutional Resource
Warren converted his son Matthew's suicide into a major institutional initiative for mental health ministry. He converted his wife Kay's HIV/AIDS encounter into the PEACE Plan. He converted his own book's commercial success into a philanthropic apparatus. Whether this represents admirable integration of personal and pastoral calling, or a pattern of deploying personal suffering for institutional ends, is a legitimate interpretive question.

### Pattern 4: Late Institutional Risk
Warren waited until retirement to ordain women — a decision he framed as defending millions of Southern Baptist women's gifts. Critics noted he had 40+ years of institutional leadership during which he did not make that move. The decision appears more costly (the SBC expulsion) in retrospect than it was in practice for Saddleback. Timing matters in assessing whether this was moral courage or managed exit.
